I have been the person building the data system and the person waiting for the report. I have been the technical lead frustrated that leadership does not understand what is possible and the operations manager frustrated that the data team cannot deliver what is needed.

Both sides think the other one does not get it. They are both right.

## The view from the technical side

When you build data systems, you see the world in terms of constraints. Data quality issues. Schema mismatches. Integration complexity. API rate limits. Processing time.

You know that the "simple report" leadership asked for requires pulling data from four systems, reconciling two different definitions of "active customer," handling missing records, and building a pipeline that runs reliably every morning at 6 AM.

You also know that leadership does not know any of this. And when you try to explain it, their eyes glaze over because they do not care about the plumbing. They just want the water to come out of the tap.

So you stop explaining. You estimate conservatively. You build what was asked for. And when leadership is disappointed by the result, you think: "If they understood what this actually takes, they would be grateful."

## The view from the business side

When you run an organization, you see the world in terms of outcomes. Revenue. Retention. Operational efficiency. Stakeholder satisfaction. Board expectations.

You know what you need to decide. You know what information would help you decide it. You do not know, and should not have to know, the technical details of making that information appear.

When you ask for a report and it takes six weeks, you wonder what the data team does all day. When the dashboard finally arrives and it does not answer your actual question, you wonder why you invested in data at all.

So you stop asking. You go back to your spreadsheets, your gut instinct, and the numbers you trust because you pulled them yourself. And when the data team is frustrated by low adoption, you think: "If they built something I could actually use, I would use it."

## The gap in between

Both narratives are true. Both are incomplete.

The technical team is right that the work is complex and underappreciated. Leadership is right that the output often misses the mark. The problem is not that either side is wrong. It is that nobody is standing in the middle, translating between the two.

This is the gap that decision infrastructure closes. Not with more meetings or better requirements documents, but with a structured system that ensures the right information reaches the right people in a form they can act on.

## What I learned from sitting on both sides

**The technical team does not need better requirements. They need better context.** Tell them what decision you are trying to make, not what chart you want to see. Show them the meeting where the data will be used, so they understand the stakes. Let them see the outcome, not just the specification.

**Leadership does not need to understand the technology. They need to trust the process.** Establish clear cadences for what gets reported, when, and why. Define metrics once and agree on them. Create a governance system so there is one version of the truth.

**Both sides need a translator.** Someone who has lived both experiences. Someone who can sit in a leadership meeting and understand the urgency, then walk into the data team's standup and translate that urgency into architecture that actually addresses it.

## Why this matters more now

AI is making this gap wider, not narrower. Technical teams are excited about what AI can do. Leadership is overwhelmed by what AI might mean. Without someone bridging that gap, organizations end up either adopting AI tools they do not need or ignoring capabilities that could transform their operations.

The same mismatch that has always existed between technical teams and business leaders is now playing out with higher stakes. The organizations that close this gap will make better decisions, faster. The ones that do not will fall further behind.
