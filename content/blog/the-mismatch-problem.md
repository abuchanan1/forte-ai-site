Here is a pattern that plays out in organizations every day.

Leadership says: "We need better visibility into our operations."

The technical team hears: "Build a dashboard."

Six weeks later, the dashboard is live. It has twelve charts, three filters, and data refreshed every hour. It is technically excellent.

Nobody uses it.

Not because the team failed. Because nobody defined what "better visibility" actually means. What decisions does the COO need to make? What data points inform those decisions? How often does the information need to refresh? What should happen when a metric goes off track?

The technical team built exactly what was asked for. The problem is that nobody asked for the right thing.

## This is the mismatch problem

The gap between what business leaders need and what technical teams build is the single biggest reason data projects fail. It is not a technology problem. It is a communication problem disguised as a technology problem.

Technical teams think in systems. They ask: What data sources are available? What is the schema? What tools should we use? How do we handle data quality?

Business leaders think in outcomes. They ask: Why are we losing clients? Are we spending too much on operations? Where should we invest next quarter?

These are not the same conversation. And without someone who understands both languages, the result is always the same: technically sound solutions that miss the point entirely.

## How the mismatch compounds

The mismatch does not just waste one project. It creates a cycle.

Leadership requests something. The technical team builds it. Leadership does not use it because it does not answer their actual question. Leadership loses trust in the data team. The data team feels undervalued and stops proactively surfacing insights. Leadership goes back to making decisions on instinct.

Each cycle reinforces the belief on both sides that the other group does not understand. And each cycle makes the next project harder to get right.

## Why requirements gathering is not the answer

The typical response to the mismatch problem is better requirements gathering. More meetings, more specification documents, more sign-offs before development begins.

This rarely works. Business leaders do not know how to specify what they need in technical terms because they should not have to. And technical teams cannot translate vague business objectives into precise specifications because the context they need lives in conversations they are not part of.

The answer is not more documentation. It is a different kind of conversation.

## Start with decisions, not deliverables

Instead of asking leadership what they want built, ask what decisions they need to make faster. Instead of specifying dashboard requirements, map the information flow from data to decision and identify where it breaks down.

This is the core of what decision infrastructure provides. It starts with the outcome and works backward to the system, rather than starting with the system and hoping it produces the right outcome.

## What this looks like in practice

A leadership team says: "We need to reduce client churn."

The wrong approach: Build a churn dashboard that shows attrition rates by segment.

The right approach: Identify the three to four leading indicators of churn. Determine who on the team can act on early warnings. Design an alert system that surfaces at-risk accounts to the right person with enough lead time to intervene. Then build the dashboard around that workflow.

The first approach gives you data. The second approach gives you decisions.
